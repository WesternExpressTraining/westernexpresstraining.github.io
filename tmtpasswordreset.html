How to Determine if an Email or Website is a Phishing Attempt

  • Check the sender's email address for discrepancies or suspicious domains.

    Example: An email claims to be from PayPal, but the sender's address is "support@paypa1.com" (note the '1' instead of 'l').

  • Look for generic greetings like "Dear Customer" instead of your name.

    Example: "Dear Customer, your account has been compromised" instead of "Dear John Smith, your account has been compromised".

  • Beware of urgent or threatening language designed to provoke an immediate response.

    Example: "Your account will be suspended within 24 hours if you do not verify your information immediately."

  • Hover over links to see the actual URL before clicking; phishing sites often have misspelled or unusual URLs.

    Example: A link appears as "www.bankofamerica.com" but when you hover over it, it shows "www.bankofamerrica.com".

  • Examine the email for spelling and grammatical errors, which are common in phishing attempts.

    Example: "Dear user, your acount has been temporaly locked. Please click here to unlock."

  • Check for unexpected attachments or requests for personal information.

    Example: An email from a supposed friend includes an unexpected attachment labeled "Invoice" or asks for your Social Security number.

  • Ensure the website has a secure connection (look for "https://" and a padlock icon).

    Example: A legitimate site should display "https://" at the beginning of the URL, indicating a secure connection. Phishing sites often lack this.
